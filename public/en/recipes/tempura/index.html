Tempura

Tempura is the dish of Edo-period street food perfected: a lace-thin batter that crackles around prawns, slices of sweet potato or kabocha, and slim Japanese eggplant. The technique arrived from Portuguese Jesuits in the 16th century but was reshaped completely on Japanese terms — ice-cold water, lumpy batter, and a fast fry that leaves the vegetables steaming inside their shell. Proper tempura is judged by the crust, not the protein. The batter should sit on the piece like lace, never coating it, and the first bite must release a faint hiss of trapped steam. Restaurants in the Ginza tradition fry one piece at a time and serve directly from the chopsticks onto the diner's plate — tempura kept waiting more than a minute is no longer tempura.

Ingredients 4 servings

  • 400 g large prawns (8–12 head-on, peeled but tails on), deveined and patted bone-dry
  • 1 small Japanese sweet potato (~200 g), peeled and cut into 5 mm rounds
  • 200 g kabocha squash, cut into 5 mm half-moons (skin left on)
  • 1 Japanese eggplant, cut on the bias into 1 cm slices
  • 6 fresh shiitake mushrooms, stems trimmed, caps scored with an X
  • 100 g shishito or thin green peppers, each pricked with a fork so they don't burst
  • For the batter (mix LAST, just before frying): 1 large egg, 250 ml ice-cold sparkling water, 150 g plain (cake) flour, sifted
  • 30 g extra plain flour for dusting the pieces before dipping
  • 1.5 L neutral oil for deep-frying (rice bran or vegetable)
  • For the tentsuyu dipping sauce: 200 ml dashi, 60 ml soy sauce, 60 ml mirin
  • 30 g daikon, finely grated and lightly squeezed
  • 10 g fresh ginger, finely grated
  • Fine sea salt and lemon wedges, to serve

How to make it

  1. 1PREP EVERYTHING DRY: pat every prawn and vegetable bone-dry — surface moisture is the enemy of crisp tempura.
  2. 2Make 3 shallow cuts on the underside of each prawn and gently press along the spine so they fry straight instead of curling.
  3. 3Spread all the cut vegetables on a tray, lightly seasoned with a pinch of salt.
  4. 4MAKE THE TENTSUYU FIRST: combine dashi, soy sauce and mirin in a small pan, bring to a brief boil to cook off the mirin's alcohol, then turn off the heat.
  5. 5Pour into small individual dipping bowls; set the grated daikon and ginger out separately so each diner stirs them in just before eating.
  6. 6HEAT THE OIL: 1.5 L of oil in a heavy pot or deep wok at 175 °C / 350 °F, measured with a probe thermometer.
  7. 7Tempura cannot be done by eye — too cool and the crust absorbs oil, too hot and it browns before the inside cooks.
  8. 8MIX THE BATTER LAST (the single most important step): in a chilled bowl, beat the egg into the ice-cold sparkling water with chopsticks.
  9. 9Tip in the sifted flour all at once and stir 5–8 strokes only.
  10. 10The batter MUST stay lumpy and you should still see streaks of dry flour — overmixing develops gluten and produces a dense, heavy crust.
  11. 11Use within 2 minutes; do not let it sit.
  12. 12FRY IN SMALL BATCHES: dust each piece very lightly with the dry flour (this helps the batter cling), dip into the batter letting excess drip back, then slip into the oil away from you.
  13. 13Never crowd — 4 or 5 pieces at a time only.
  14. 14Cook prawns 90 seconds, sweet potato 3 minutes, eggplant and kabocha 2 minutes, shiitake 90 seconds, shishito 60 seconds.
  15. 15The crust should stay pale ivory and never brown.
  16. 16DRAIN AND SERVE IMMEDIATELY: lift each piece onto a wire rack lined with paper (never paper alone — the bottom steams and softens).
  17. 17Bring to the table within 60 seconds.
  18. 18Diners can dip into the tentsuyu with daikon and ginger stirred in, or eat the vegetables with a pinch of fine salt and a squeeze of lemon.
💡
Tip: Taste before serving and adjust seasoning and salt to your liking.

Clam Chowder

Clam chowder is the emblematic soup of New England — a creamy, thick chowder with fresh clams, potatoes, smoked bacon, and onion, enriched with milk and cream. There is a centuries-old culinary war: New England Clam Chowder (white, with cream) versus Manhattan Clam Chowder (red, with tomatoes), and Bostonians consider the tomato version nearly an offense. Clam chowder is traditionally served in a hollowed-out sourdough bread bowl — an edible bowl that absorbs the soup. It is Boston's harbor food, present at every restaurant along the coast.

Ingredients 4 servings

  • 1.5 kg fresh littleneck or quahog clams in shells (or 2 × 200 g cans whole baby clams in juice as substitute for landlocked cooks)
  • 500 ml fresh clam juice or fish stock (reserve all the steaming liquid if using fresh clams)
  • 150 g thick-cut smoked bacon, diced 0.5 cm
  • 1 large yellow onion (~250 g), finely diced
  • 2 medium celery stalks (~120 g), finely diced
  • 4 garlic cloves, minced (optional — Boston purists frown but most modern recipes include)
  • 30 g unsalted butter
  • 30 g all-purpose flour (for the roux)
  • 750 g floury potatoes (Yukon Gold or russet), peeled and cut into 1.5 cm cubes
  • 250 ml whole milk
  • 250 ml heavy cream (or half-and-half for a lighter version)
  • 2 bay leaves + 1 tsp dried thyme + 0.5 tsp freshly ground black pepper
  • 0.5 tsp salt (taste first — clam juice is salty)
  • A pinch of cayenne or 1 dash hot sauce (optional)
  • 1 small bunch fresh chives, finely chopped, and 2 tbsp fresh flat-leaf parsley to garnish
  • To serve: 4 small hollowed-out sourdough rolls as edible bread bowls OR oyster crackers (a traditional New England side); 1 lemon, sliced into wedges

How to make it

  1. 1STEAM THE CLAMS: rinse 1.5 kg fresh clams in cold water 2–3 times to remove sand.
  2. 2Discard any that are open and do not close when tapped.
  3. 3Place them in a wide pot with 250 ml water, cover and steam over high heat 4–5 minutes until the shells just open.
  4. 4Discard any that stayed shut.
  5. 5Lift the clams out, leaving the steaming liquid behind.
  6. 6Strain that liquid through a fine sieve lined with damp cheesecloth into a measuring cup — you should have ~400–500 ml of clean clam broth.
  7. 7Pick the clam meat from the shells, roughly chop, and set aside.
  8. 8(If using canned clams, just drain reserving the juice, and use the 500 ml fish stock + the canned juice.) 2.
  9. 9RENDER THE BACON: in a heavy 5 L pot or Dutch oven over medium heat, add 150 g diced bacon.
  10. 10Cook stirring often 6–8 minutes until the bacon is deeply browned and most of the fat has rendered.
  11. 11Lift the crispy bacon out with a slotted spoon onto a paper towel and set aside for garnish.
  12. 12Leave 3 tbsp of bacon fat in the pot.
  13. 13SWEAT THE AROMATICS: add 250 g diced onion + 120 g diced celery to the bacon fat with a pinch of salt.
  14. 14Cook stirring often 8–10 minutes until soft and translucent but not browned — chowder is white, you do not want colour here.
  15. 15Add 4 minced garlic cloves and cook 1 minute more.
  16. 16THE ROUX: add 30 g butter and let it melt.
  17. 17Sprinkle 30 g flour over the vegetables and stir constantly with a wooden spoon 2 minutes, cooking the raw flour taste out without letting it darken — this is a white roux.
  18. 18ADD LIQUID GRADUALLY: pour in the strained clam broth in a slow steady stream while whisking constantly to prevent lumps.
  19. 19Add 750 g potato cubes + 2 bay leaves + 1 tsp thyme + 0.5 tsp black pepper.
  20. 20Bring to a gentle simmer.
  21. 21SIMMER POTATOES: cook uncovered 15–18 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the potatoes are tender but still hold their shape — pierce one with a knife and it should slide in with no resistance.
  22. 22ADD DAIRY AND CLAMS: lower the heat to the lowest setting.
  23. 23Stir in 250 ml whole milk and 250 ml heavy cream — do NOT let it boil after adding dairy, or the cream will curdle.
  24. 24Warm through 3 minutes.
  25. 25Stir in the chopped clam meat and most of the bacon (save 2 tbsp of bacon and all the chives for garnish).
  26. 26Simmer 3 more minutes until the clams are just heated through — overcooking turns them tough and rubbery.
  27. 27Remove the bay leaves.
  28. 28Taste and adjust seasoning with salt and a pinch of cayenne if you like.
  29. 29SERVE IN BREAD BOWLS: hollow out 4 small sourdough rolls (or use oyster crackers as a traditional simpler option).
  30. 30Ladle the hot chowder into the bread bowls or deep soup plates.
  31. 31Top each with the reserved crispy bacon, a generous scatter of chives and parsley, and a fresh twist of black pepper.
  32. 32Serve immediately with lemon wedges on the side.
  33. 33A New England restaurant typically brings a small dish of oyster crackers and a side of hot sauce for the table.
💡
Tip: Fish is ready when the flesh turns opaque and flakes easily with a fork.

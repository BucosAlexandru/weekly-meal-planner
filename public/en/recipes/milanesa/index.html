Milanesa

Milanesa arrived in Argentina with the Italian immigrants of the late 19th and early 20th centuries, carried directly from the cotoletta alla Milanese of Lombardy. In its Argentine form, a thin slice of beef вЂ” or sometimes chicken or veal вЂ” is pounded flat, dredged in beaten egg and breadcrumbs, and fried in oil until a uniform golden crust forms. The process is quick, the result consistent: a crisp exterior around tender meat. It became one of the most common everyday meals in Argentina, eaten at school canteens, family tables, and corner restaurants with equal frequency. The napolitana variant tops the fried cutlet with tomato sauce, ham, and melted cheese. In sandwich form вЂ” tucked into a bread roll with lettuce and tomato вЂ” it is one of Argentina's most popular street foods. Lemon juice squeezed over the crust just before eating is standard.

Onigiri

Onigiri are older than sushi: rice balls shaped into triangles, discs, or barrels with a salted plum, grilled fish, or seaweed at the centre have been packed into Japanese lunchboxes since at least the Heian period. The triangle — sanrokutai — has a practical origin: it stacks neatly in a bento box, fits the hand, and offers three flat faces that a strip of nori can wrap around without slipping. The defining technique is mochizukuri — pressing the rice firmly enough to hold its shape without compacting it into paste. A good onigiri yields to a slight squeeze yet stays whole; a bad one is either too loose (it falls apart in the lunchbox) or too dense (chewy and gluey). Salt on the hands, never on the rice — the seasoning lives on the outer skin, not throughout the grain.

Ingredients 4 servings

  • 500 g Japanese short-grain rice (Koshihikari or sushi rice)
  • 600 ml fresh cold water for cooking (rice to water ratio ~1:1.1)
  • For the tezu (finger water): 200 ml warm water + 1.5 tsp fine sea salt
  • 4 sheets toasted nori, cut into strips about 3 cm wide × the length of the sheet
  • Filling option 1 — tuna mayo: 80 g canned tuna (drained), 1.5 tbsp Japanese mayonnaise, ½ tsp soy sauce
  • Filling option 2 — umeboshi: 60 g pitted salted plums, lightly mashed with a fork
  • Filling option 3 — shiozake (grilled salted salmon): 100 g salmon fillet grilled and flaked, with the skin removed
  • Filling option 4 — okaka: 8 g bonito flakes mixed with 1 tsp soy sauce
  • For plain onigiri: 2 tbsp furikake (rice seasoning), to coat the outside
  • Optional: a few sesame seeds for the surface of plain ones

How to make it

  1. 1RINSE AND COOK THE RICE: rinse the 500 g rice in cold water 4 or 5 times, swirling with your hand, until the water runs almost clear.
  2. 2Drain in a strainer for 20 minutes.
  3. 3Combine with 600 ml fresh water in a heavy pot or rice cooker.
  4. 4Bring to a boil, cover, drop to the lowest heat, and cook 12 minutes (or on the rice-cooker programme).
  5. 5Rest off-heat, lid still on, another 10 minutes — the carry-over steam is what makes the grain perfect, do not lift the lid early.
  6. 6PREPARE THE FILLINGS: while the rice rests, prepare your chosen fillings in small bowls.
  7. 7Mash the umeboshi with a fork.
  8. 8Mix canned tuna with Japanese mayonnaise to a creamy paste.
  9. 9Flake the grilled salmon.
  10. 10Each onigiri takes only about 1 tablespoon of filling — too much and the rice can't seal around it.
  11. 11MAKE THE TEZU: in a small bowl, dissolve 1.5 tsp fine salt in 200 ml warm water.
  12. 12This is your finger water — you'll dip both palms into it before shaping every onigiri, so the rice doesn't stick and the outer skin gets a delicate salting.
  13. 13SHAPE THE ONIGIRI: tip the hot rice onto a wide tray or wooden board and gently fluff with a paddle, then let it cool until just warm to the touch — too hot steams your palms, too cold and the rice won't bind.
  14. 14Wet your palms in the tezu.
  15. 15Take about 100 g of rice (a generous handful), pat flat on your palm, place 1 tbsp filling in the centre, then cup your hand and gather the rice around the filling.
  16. 16Press into a triangle with three flat faces — firm but gentle pressure, never compacted into a brick.
  17. 17FINISH WITH NORI: wrap a strip of nori around the base of each triangle just before eating, so the seaweed stays crisp.
  18. 18If packing for a lunchbox, store the nori separately and wrap on opening.
  19. 19For plain onigiri (no filling), roll the outside in furikake instead of using nori.
  20. 20SERVE FRESH: eat at room temperature within 4 hours.
  21. 21Properly made onigiri travel beautifully and are the original Japanese packed lunch — better-than-bread for any portable rice meal.
💡
Tip: Taste before serving and adjust seasoning and salt to your liking.

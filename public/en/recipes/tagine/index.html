Tagine

Tagine takes its name from the conical earthenware vessel in which it is cooked — a design that has remained essentially unchanged across centuries of Moroccan and North African cooking. The cone traps rising steam and returns moisture to the ingredients below, producing meat that falls apart without drying out and a concentrated sauce that needs no thickening. Lamb is the most traditional protein, though chicken and fish tagines are equally common. What makes Moroccan tagine distinctive is the interplay of savoury and sweet: preserved lemons, olives, dried apricots, or prunes appear alongside warm spices such as ras el hanout, cumin, and ginger. The slow pace of cooking and the vessel itself are as much part of the recipe as any single ingredient.

Ingredients 4 servings

  • 1kg bone-in lamb shoulder, cut into 4 cm cubes (or chicken thighs)
  • 2 large onions, thinly sliced
  • 4 garlic cloves, minced
  • 30g fresh ginger, grated
  • 2 tsp ras el hanout (Moroccan spice blend)
  • 1 tsp ground cumin
  • 1 tsp ground cinnamon
  • 1/2 tsp ground turmeric
  • 1/4 tsp saffron threads (steeped in 2 tbsp warm water)
  • 200g dried apricots (or prunes)
  • 200g canned chickpeas, drained
  • 2 carrots, cut into thick batons
  • 1 preserved lemon, peel only (or juice of 1 fresh lemon)
  • 3 tbsp olive oil
  • 1 tsp salt
  • 500ml chicken stock or water
  • 1 tbsp honey
  • 30g fresh coriander, chopped
  • 30g fresh parsley, chopped
  • 30g toasted almonds (for garnish)
  • Couscous or Moroccan flatbread, to serve

How to make it

  1. 1Season the lamb cubes with salt, half the ras el hanout, cumin, cinnamon, and turmeric.
  2. 2Let rest for 30 minutes so the flavours penetrate.
  3. 3Heat 3 tbsp olive oil in a tagine vessel (or heavy-bottomed pot) over medium heat.
  4. 4Add the meat in a single layer and brown for 4–5 minutes per side.
  5. 5Lift out onto a plate.
  6. 6In the same pot, sauté the sliced onions for 8–10 minutes, until soft and lightly golden.
  7. 7Add the garlic, grated ginger, and remaining spices.
  8. 8Cook 1 minute.
  9. 9Pour in the saffron water and 500ml stock.
  10. 10Return the meat with its juices and add the carrots.
  11. 11Bring to a simmer, then cover the tagine.
  12. 12Reduce heat to the lowest setting and braise for 1h 30min–2 hours (1 hour for chicken), until the meat is falling off the bone.
  13. 13Do not lift the lid often — the steam should stay trapped.
  14. 14Add the dried apricots, chickpeas, and preserved lemon peel.
  15. 15Cook covered for another 20–25 minutes.
  16. 16Turn off the heat.
  17. 17Drizzle the honey over the tagine and stir gently — the dried fruit will provide the traditional sweetness.
  18. 18Taste, adjust salt.
  19. 19Scatter half the coriander and parsley on top.
  20. 20Serve straight from the tagine vessel with toasted almonds sprinkled over and the rest of the herbs, alongside couscous or Moroccan flatbread.
💡
Tip: Let the meat rest for 5 minutes after cooking before slicing or serving.
